Monks in the City

Originally founded to serve the German-speaking immigrants of Newark, the abbey is located in what is now the center of New Jersey’s largest city. While it is true that most monasteries are located in more rural or even isolated areas, there is also a long-standing tradition of monasteries being located in major cities, especially in Europe. Again, while there is a monastic tradition that stresses an enclosed cloister and strong a contemplative element, there is a second stream of monasticism that is more pastorally oriented, allowing for more contact with the “world,” for example in staffing schools and parishes. Newark Abbey stands in this latter tradition. When a monk feels the need for a period of peace and quiet he can spend some time at Munsee, our 77-acre wooded property in the mountains about an hour from the monastery. [Photo of Munsee; caption: When a monk feels the need for a little extra peace and quiet he can spend some retreat time on our seventy-two acres of wooded property in the mountains about an hour from the monastery]

We have responded to the changing needs of the Church of Newark, the city and the surrounding area over the years. The monks staff the abbey’s parish, Saint Mary’s, and assist on weekends in a couple of other parishes, and serve as chaplains for a nearby convent of Benedictine Sisters. A major focus of our common work is Saint Benedict’s Preparatory School for boys, founded in 1868. The school enjoys a fine reputation in academics and athletics, and has provided many vocations to the Benedictines and to the diocesan clergy. Since the school’s population has always reflected the changing racial composition of the city--including German, Irish and Italian—today a majority of its 550 students are African-American and Hispanic, while a significant number come from the Portuguese community.

We see our location in the center of the city as both a unique privilege and a God-given opportunity to proclaim to our neighbors the good news of God’s constant saving presence in the world. Our life of quiet and silence speaks loudly to a world that is overwhelmed with noise. Our life in community sends a powerful message to a city often divided by strife. Our life of simplicity and common ownership speaks to a world that often loses its way in the pursuit of material wealth. Our life of obedience and celibacy challenges the basic assumptions of a culture based on egocentrism and selfish pleasure-seeking. Our regular schedule of communal and private prayer is a leaven in the chaos and confusion of a world that has forgotten God.
